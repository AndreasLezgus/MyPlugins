# [Title: Direct, specific, benefit-driven | 50–60 chars]

*[Subtitle: One sentence that complements the title and earns the click.]*

---

[OPENING – No "In this issue" formulas. No subject line repetition.
Start directly with the observation, tension, or question.

Pattern: Something I've seen. The uncomfortable truth it reveals.

Examples:
"Forty years in regulated environments taught me one thing about AI strategy:
the hard questions don't disappear just because you ignore them in the kickoff meeting."

"Pilots impress. Demos convince. Budgets flow.
So why do most AI initiatives look very different eighteen months later?"

"There's a pattern I've noticed across every AI project I've been involved with.
It has nothing to do with the technology."]

[Paragraph 2: Establish the stakes. Why does this matter now?
What's the cost of getting this wrong? 3–5 sentences.
Keep it concrete – not abstract. Numbers help.]

---

[SECTION HEADING: The core tension or problem]

[THE ANALYSIS – This is where the "second opinion" lives.

What the conventional wisdom says:
Reference Gartner or the source briefly: "According to Gartner's [Title] ([ID], [Year])…"
Paraphrase, don't quote at length. One direct quote maximum, under 15 words.

What practice shows:
"In practice, however…" / "What this analysis misses…"
Bring in the concrete example from regulated environments.
Anonymous but specific: "In a large public sector organization with [X] staff…"

The uncomfortable truth:
3–5 sentences. This is where the edge goes.
Diplomatically sharp – not angry, not resigned.]

---

[SECTION HEADING: What this actually looks like]

[PRACTICAL ANCHOR – The concrete example, fully developed.

What was the situation?
What happened?
What was the result?

Short sentences. 3–4 sentences per paragraph.
"The outcome was predictable." is a valid sentence here.

Note on context for international readers:
German public sector context needs one sentence of framing:
"Germany's public sector operates under strict data sovereignty and procurement rules
that make the typical SaaS-first approach legally problematic."]

---

[SECTION HEADING: What you can actually do]

[RECOMMENDATIONS – 3–4 concrete steps.

One sentence introduction before any list.

**First:** [Specific action + brief rationale. 2 sentences.]

**Second:** [Specific action + brief rationale. 2 sentences.]

**Third:** [Specific action + brief rationale. 2 sentences.]

One sentence transition after the list.]

---

[CLOSING – Restate the core message differently.
Invite a response or reflection – Substack lives on replies.
End with dry humor or a sharp aphorism.

Allowed closing aphorisms (EN):
- "Write your AI strategy now — before AI writes it for you."
- "Experience isn't a liability. In regulated environments, it's the only thing that works."
- "The hard part isn't the technology. It never was."

Closing invitation (Substack-specific):
"If this resonates — or if you'd push back on any of it — I'd genuinely like to hear it.
Hit reply."
